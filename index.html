The Impact of Rainfall on Agriculture and Local Inflation

This visualization explores the chain reaction between climate, agriculture, and economics across Malaysian states and territories. By examining historical rainfall patterns and monsoon seasons, we can identify how weather intensity impacts the production volumes of essential food and industrial crops. In turn, these agricultural shifts directly influence local living costs, allowing us to track consumer price index volatility and understand how environmental factors ultimately drive regional inflation over time.

Sarawak, Kelantan, and Negeri Sembilan record
the heaviest annual rainfall in Malaysia. This
consistent high precipitation shapes local
land conditions and directly dictates
agricultural potential.
The standard January to December sequence
shows the Northeast Monsoon bringing
intense rain to regions like Labuan and
Kelantan during November and December.
National crop production remains stable.
Paddy, fruits, and vegetables form the
largest volumes, ensuring steady food
supplies despite minor yearly fluctuations.
Viewing individual state trajectories reveals
unique growth patterns. Some regions show
steady production increases, reflecting
strong agricultural focus and development.
States dedicating more land to farming
generally produce higher volumes.
However, yield efficiency varies greatly
depending on the chosen crop and
farming practices.
Higher rainfall does not guarantee better
harvests. Specific crops thrive only under
ideal weather, showing clear mismatches
between heavy rain and total yield.
Certain states rely heavily on a single crop.
Kedah, Penang, and Perlis focus almost
entirely on paddy, while others maintain
diverse agricultural output.
The inflation gap widens during periods
of economic stress. This indicates that
prices for daily goods move unevenly,
creating unpredictable living costs.
Every state experienced rising living costs
between the selected years. Selangor shows
the largest index jump, placing heavier
financial pressure on its residents.
Top producing states anchor the
national food supply. Observing
their local inflation reveals how
regional agricultural output interacts
with rising consumer prices.
